## Introduction

Prostate cancer is the second most common malignancy in men worldwide, with an estimated 1.4 million new cases annually [1]. While advances in early detection and treatment have substantially improved long-term survival, prostate cancer patients remain at considerable risk of acute clinical deterioration during hospital admissions—whether for cancer-related complications, treatment sequelae, or unrelated acute illnesses [2,3].

Existing prognostic tools in prostate cancer, such as the D'Amico risk classification, CAPRA score, and AJCC staging system, are designed to predict long-term oncologic outcomes including biochemical recurrence, metastasis, and cancer-specific mortality [4–6]. These tools rely heavily on histopathological features (Gleason grade, TNM stage) and serum PSA levels. However, they were not developed to predict short-term acute outcomes during hospitalization, a setting where immediate clinical decisions depend more on acute physiological status than on tumor biology [7].

In recent years, machine learning (ML) methods applied to prostate cancer have predominantly focused on SEER (Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results) registry data for long-term survival prediction [8–11]. A systematic review of 47 studies by Ahuja et al. (2024) identified XGBoost and CatBoost as the best-performing algorithms, with AUCs typically ranging from 0.85 to 0.94 for 5–10 year survival outcomes [12]. However, these SEER-based models share a common limitation: they use static cancer registry variables and cannot leverage the rich temporal data available in electronic health records (EHRs). Critically, no prior study has applied ML to EHR data for short-term acute outcome prediction in hospitalized prostate cancer patients.

The MIMIC-IV (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care) database provides a unique opportunity to address this gap. As a publicly available EHR database containing detailed laboratory, medication, and administrative data from over 380,000 hospital admissions at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (2008–2022), it enables the development of clinically deployable prediction models using features routinely available at the point of care [13,14].

In this study, we aimed to: (1) develop and internally validate an XGBoost-based ML model to predict 30-day mortality or ICU admission in hospitalized prostate cancer patients using MIMIC-IV EHR data; (2) compare its performance against standard logistic regression; (3) identify the most important predictive features using SHAP analysis; and (4) evaluate model performance across clinically relevant subgroups.
