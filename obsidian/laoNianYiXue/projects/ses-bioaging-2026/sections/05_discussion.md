# Discussion

In 7,810 Chinese older adults, biological age differences were concentrated across grip strength strata rather than SES levels. The overall ATE was modest (−0.118 SD, 95% CI: −0.146 to −0.091), with biological age lower in the low-SES group—a finding that may reflect survivorship bias (frailer low-SES individuals being less likely to survive to age 60), the obesity paradox in older Asian populations, or residual confounding. The 0.73 SD gradient across SES × grip strength categories was driven primarily by grip strength; notably, low-SES participants with preserved grip strength showed the most favorable biological age profile (−0.25), undermining the "double jeopardy" hypothesis that predicts synergistic effects of socioeconomic and physiological vulnerability [18,22]. Prior CHARLS-based studies have shown that childhood socioeconomic position predicts grip strength trajectories decades later [4], and that SES disparities in aging metrics are larger in China than in Western populations [5].

The rural-specific reversal of the SES–biological age association (CATE = +0.070 in rural vs. −0.044 in urban) is noteworthy. In rural areas, low SES was associated with higher biological age, consistent with the conventional SES-health gradient, whereas in urban areas the pattern reversed and the association was not statistically significant (95% CI crossed zero). This urban–rural divergence may reflect differential survivorship bias (stronger selection effects in urban areas with greater SES inequality), differences in the meaning of SES indicators across settings, or urban-specific lifestyle factors associated with higher SES that adversely affect biological aging (e.g., sedentary behavior, dietary changes) [16].

Contrary to evidence from Western populations, depressive symptoms (CES-D) showed a significant indirect effect on the SES–biological aging pathway (indirect effect: +0.012, 95% CI: +0.008 to +0.016). However, the estimated proportion mediated (63%) had wide bootstrap confidence intervals (24% to >100%) because the total effect was small and imprecisely estimated (95% CI: −0.007 to +0.046), indicating that the SES–biological aging association after adjustment was not statistically significant. This pattern—a significant indirect effect in the presence of a non-significant total effect—is consistent with inconsistent mediation and warrants cautious interpretation. Chinese older adults may express distress differently than Western populations [21], and allostatic load research suggests that inflammatory and metabolic pathways may operate alongside affective symptoms [9,26,27]. Alternative mediators—nutrition, healthcare access, occupational exposures—warrant further investigation [28].

Five limitations warrant discussion. The cross-sectional design prevents causal interpretation; the CATE estimates reported here are conditional associations. The median-split SES classification discards within-group variation and may misclassify individuals near the cutoff. The biological age composite is a pragmatic index of available clinical markers and omits molecular measures (DNA methylation, inflammatory biomarkers) that could reveal deeper biological mechanisms [29]. Missing biomarker data were substantial (26–31% across markers) and were handled with median imputation, which may underestimate standard errors; complete-case sensitivity analysis yielded consistent results. The E-value of 1.47 from the unadjusted model (bootstrap 95% CI lower bound: 1.41; adjusted model E-value: 1.15, CI lower bound: 1.00) indicates limited robustness to unmeasured confounding. Finally, CHARLS income data contain a high proportion of zero values, particularly in rural participants; the log(1+x) transformation mitigates but does not resolve this issue.

These findings suggest several directions for future work. First, longitudinal designs with repeated SES and biomarker measures are needed to establish temporal ordering [4,28]. Second, integrating molecular biomarkers would clarify whether the SES–physical function interaction observed here reflects inflammatory, metabolic, or epigenetic pathways [3,8,26]. Third, cross-national comparisons using harmonized data from CHARLS, ELSA, HRS, and other aging cohorts could test the generalizability of the SES–biology interaction identified here [5,23,34].

## Conclusions

Biological age differences in this cohort of Chinese older adults were concentrated across grip strength strata rather than SES levels. Grip strength—a simple clinical measure—may identify older adults at elevated biological aging risk irrespective of socioeconomic position. The unexpected finding that high-SES groups showed marginally higher biological aging warrants further investigation in longitudinal designs and may reflect survivorship bias or cohort-specific factors in this population.
