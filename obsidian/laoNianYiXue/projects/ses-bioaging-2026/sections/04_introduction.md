# Introduction

Low socioeconomic status predicts morbidity and mortality in older adults independent of established biomedical risk factors [1]. Reducing health inequities in aging populations has been recognized as a global priority [2]. The mechanisms through which socioeconomic disadvantage becomes biologically embedded—the process by which social inequality "gets under the skin"—remain only partially characterized [3].

Associations between SES and individual biomarkers of aging are well documented, including grip strength [4], gait speed [5], and inflammatory markers [6]. Three gaps limit translation of this evidence. First, most research treats SES as a covariate to be adjusted for, rather than the exposure of primary interest [7]. Second, the population subgroups in which SES exerts its strongest biological effects are not well defined—understanding this heterogeneity would inform intervention targeting [8]. Third, evidence from Chinese populations is sparse relative to Western cohorts [9].

The China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) captures socioeconomic data alongside physical function and psychological assessments in a nationally representative sample of Chinese adults aged ≥45 [10]. The urban–rural divide in income, education, and healthcare access—combined with China's recent economic transformation—generates SES variation suited to studying the biological embedding of inequality [11].

We aimed to (1) quantify the heterogeneous effects of low SES on biological aging across population subgroups defined by physical function, psychological status, and urban/rural residence; (2) evaluate whether psychological distress mediates the SES–biological aging pathway; and (3) assess the robustness of these associations to unmeasured confounding. We hypothesized that the biological cost of low SES would be most pronounced among individuals with compromised physical function—a "double jeopardy" of socioeconomic and physiological vulnerability.
