Dear Editor,

We submit our manuscript entitled **"SES Heterogeneity in Biological Aging: A CHARLS Cohort Study of Chinese Older Adults"** for consideration for publication in *Geriatrics & Gerontology International*.

Reducing health inequities in aging populations is a global priority, yet the population subgroups that bear the greatest biological burden of socioeconomic disadvantage remain poorly characterized. In this cross-sectional analysis of 7,810 participants aged ≥60 from the nationally representative China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) 2013 wave, we quantified heterogeneous effects of low socioeconomic status (SES) on a composite biological age score across subgroups defined by physical function, psychological distress, and urban/rural residence. We estimated conditional average treatment effects (CATE) with bootstrap confidence intervals across prespecified subgroups, tested mediation through depressive symptoms (CES-D), and calculated E-values to assess sensitivity to unmeasured confounding.

Our findings demonstrate that biological age differences were concentrated across grip strength strata rather than SES levels, with a 0.73 SD gradient across SES × grip strength categories that was driven primarily by grip strength. Low-SES participants with preserved grip strength showed the most favorable biological age profile, undermining the "double jeopardy" hypothesis. The SES effect direction reversed in rural residents (CATE = +0.070; 95% CI: +0.034, +0.102) compared to urban residents (CATE = −0.044; 95% CI: −0.101, +0.013). Depressive symptoms showed a significant indirect effect (+0.012; 95% CI: +0.008, +0.016) on the SES–biological aging pathway, although the total effect was modest and imprecise. The E-value of 1.47 (bootstrap 95% CI lower bound: 1.41) indicated limited robustness to unmeasured confounding. Grip strength—a simple, low-cost clinical measure—may help identify older adults at elevated biological aging risk irrespective of socioeconomic position.

We believe this manuscript is well suited for *Geriatrics & Gerontology International* for three reasons. First, the journal's focus on aging populations in the Asia-Pacific region aligns directly with our use of CHARLS, a nationally representative Chinese cohort. Second, the interdisciplinary scope—bridging epidemiology, clinical geriatrics, and social determinants of health—matches the journal's category structure. Third, recent CHARLS-based studies on SES and aging trajectories published in the geriatrics literature demonstrate the timeliness of this topic.

This manuscript has not been published and is not under consideration for publication elsewhere. All authors have read and approved the manuscript and declare no conflicts of interest. The CHARLS study was approved by the Biomedical Ethics Committee of Peking University (IRB00001052-11015), and all participants provided written informed consent.
