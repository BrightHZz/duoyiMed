## Abstract

**Aim:** Whether socioeconomic status (SES) predicts the rate of biological aging—as distinct from its cross-sectional level—remains unclear. We tested whether baseline SES predicts the two-year trajectory of a composite biological age score in Chinese older adults.

**Methods:** We conducted a longitudinal analysis of 4,658 participants aged ≥60 from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), using baseline (2013) and follow-up (2015) waves. Baseline SES was a five-component composite (education, urban/rural residence, per-capita income, expenditure, household assets), dichotomized at the median. The outcome was two-year change in a four-marker biological age composite (grip strength, gait speed, BMI, systolic blood pressure; Z-scored using baseline reference). We estimated conditional average treatment effects (CATE) of low baseline SES on biological age change across prespecified subgroups, tested mediation through change in depressive symptoms (CES-D), and computed E-values.

**Results:** Mean biological age change was −0.053 SD (SD = 0.485) over two years. The overall CATE of low baseline SES on biological age change was +0.003 SD (bootstrap 95% CI: −0.025, +0.033), indicating no detectable effect. All subgroup CATE estimates had 95% confidence intervals crossing zero, including urban (+0.019; 95% CI: −0.039, +0.070), rural (−0.001; 95% CI: −0.039, +0.035), low grip (+0.010; 95% CI: −0.026, +0.051), and high grip (−0.019; 95% CI: −0.057, +0.021). Change in CES-D did not mediate the SES–bioaging trajectory association (indirect effect: +0.0004; 95% CI: −0.0005, +0.0015; proportion mediated: 2.9%). E-values ranged from 1.05 to 1.13, indicating minimal robustness to unmeasured confounding.

**Conclusions:** In a nationally representative Chinese cohort, baseline SES did not predict two-year biological aging trajectory. Baseline grip strength strata showed substantial regression to the mean but SES did not differentiate trajectories within grip strata, suggesting that physical function rather than socioeconomic position is the more proximal correlate of short-term biological aging change in older adults.

**Keywords:** Biological aging; CHARLS; Grip strength; Health inequality; Longitudinal; Socioeconomic status

## Introduction

Low socioeconomic status (SES) is associated with increased morbidity and mortality in older adults [1,35]. The hypothesis that socioeconomic disadvantage becomes biologically embedded—"getting under the skin"—has driven extensive research on the SES–health gradient [3,25]. However, the predominantly cross-sectional evidence base leaves a fundamental question unanswered: does low SES predict the *rate* at which individuals age biologically, or is it merely associated with the *level* of biological aging already attained?

Cross-sectional associations between SES and biomarkers of aging, including grip strength [4,6,23], gait speed [7], and allostatic load [9,10,26], are well documented. Most studies, however, treat SES as a covariate rather than the exposure of primary interest [12,28], and few have examined whether SES predicts within-person change in biological aging markers over time. This distinction between level and rate is critical for causal inference and intervention targeting: if low SES accelerates biological aging, then policies addressing socioeconomic inequality would be expected to slow aging trajectories. If, conversely, the cross-sectional SES–bioaging association reflects confounding, survivorship bias, or reverse causation, the case for SES-targeted interventions as anti-aging strategies would be substantially weakened.

Evidence from Chinese populations is particularly important given China's rapid demographic transition, marked socioeconomic gradients, and the urban–rural divide in income, education, and healthcare access [4,5,16,17]. Prior CHARLS-based studies have demonstrated that childhood socioeconomic position predicts grip strength trajectories decades later [4] and that SES disparities in aging metrics are larger in China than in Western populations [5]. However, whether adult SES measured at a single time point predicts subsequent short-term biological aging trajectory in this population is unknown.

We used the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) 2013 baseline and 2015 follow-up waves to address three objectives: (1) to test whether baseline low SES predicts two-year change in a composite biological age score; (2) to evaluate whether change in depressive symptoms (CES-D) mediates the SES–biological aging trajectory association; and (3) to assess the sensitivity of these longitudinal associations to unmeasured confounding. We also examined whether SES and baseline physical function interact in predicting biological aging trajectory, evaluating the "double jeopardy" hypothesis that socioeconomic and physiological vulnerability act synergistically [18,22].

## Methods

### Study Design and Population

We conducted a prospective cohort analysis using the 2013 (baseline) and 2015 (follow-up) waves of CHARLS, reported in accordance with the STROBE guidelines. CHARLS is a nationally representative survey of Chinese adults aged ≥45, employing multistage stratified probability-proportional-to-size sampling [15,33]. We included participants aged ≥60 at baseline (2013) who had biomarker data available in both waves. Of 5,864 eligible participants aged ≥60 at baseline, 4,658 (79.4%) were successfully followed up in 2015 and constituted the analytical sample. CHARLS missing value codes (≥900) were replaced with NaN. Missing biomarker values were imputed using median imputation; variable-specific missing rates before imputation were 1.4–6.2% (Supplementary Table S1), substantially lower than the cross-sectional analysis because the longitudinal sample was restricted to participants with measurements in both waves.

### Socioeconomic Status — Baseline Exposure

Baseline SES (2013) was a composite index from five dimensions: education years, urban/rural residence, per-capita household income (sum of 13 income sources divided by household size), per-capita expenditure (total household expenditure / household size), and household assets. Monetary variables were log(1+x) transformed. The five Z-score standardized components were averaged into a continuous SES score, dichotomized at the median into low-SES and high-SES groups.

### Biological Aging — Longitudinal Outcome

A composite biological age score was constructed from four markers: maximum grip strength (kg, mean of four measurements), gait speed (m/s, 2.5 m walk), BMI (kg/m²), and systolic blood pressure (mmHg). Grip strength and gait speed were reverse-coded so that higher scores indicate more advanced biological aging. Markers were Z-scored using the baseline (2013) mean and standard deviation as the reference, ensuring that changes in the composite reflected absolute biological change rather than shifts in the sample distribution. The two-year change in biological age (Δbio-age = bio-age<sub>2015</sub> − bio-age<sub>2013</sub>) was the primary outcome. CES-D was excluded from the composite to avoid circularity with the mediation analysis.

### Covariates

Analyses adjusted for baseline age (continuous), sex, and baseline biological age (to account for regression to the mean). Subgroup analyses stratified by baseline grip strength (median split, 27.8 kg), baseline CES-D (median split), urban/rural residence, and baseline age group (60–69, ≥70).

### Statistical Analysis

**Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE).** The overall ATE was the mean difference in Δbio-age between low-SES and high-SES groups. Within each prespecified subgroup, CATE was calculated similarly. Percentile bootstrap 95% confidence intervals (1,000 resamples) were computed for all CATE estimates. Positive CATE values indicate that low SES is associated with faster biological aging (greater increase in bio-age) over two years.

**Mediation Analysis.** We tested whether two-year change in depressive symptoms (ΔCES-D = CES-D<sub>2015</sub> − CES-D<sub>2013</sub>) mediated the SES–Δbio-age association using the product-of-coefficients method [30,32]. Path a: baseline SES score → ΔCES-D (adjusted for baseline age, sex, and baseline CES-D). Path b: ΔCES-D → Δbio-age (adjusted for SES, baseline age, sex, and baseline bio-age). Bootstrap 95% CIs (1,000 resamples) were computed for all mediation parameters.

**E-value.** The E-value quantifies the minimum strength of association an unmeasured confounder would need with both exposure and outcome to fully explain the observed association [31]. We computed E-values from both the unadjusted ATE and the adjusted linear regression coefficient.

**SES × Grip Interaction.** Participants were cross-classified by baseline SES (high/low) and baseline grip strength (high/low, median split). Mean Δbio-age values were compared across the four groups.

**Sensitivity Analysis.** We repeated the primary analysis using: (a) complete-case analysis without imputation; (b) SES as a continuous variable per SD increase; and (c) baseline-referenced Z-scores that included the 2013-only participants (N = 5,864) in the reference distribution.

Analyses used Python 3.12 with pandas (v2.0) and NumPy (v1.24).

## Results

### Study Population and Attrition

Of 5,864 CHARLS participants aged ≥60 at baseline (2013), 4,658 (79.4%) were retained at follow-up (2015). The analytical sample had a mean baseline age of 67.8 ± 6.4 years, 50% female, 35% urban, and mean education of 5.9 years. Median per-capita monthly income was 100 yuan. Attrition analysis showed that participants lost to follow-up were older (mean 71.4 vs. 67.8 years, p < 0.001) and had lower baseline grip strength (26.8 vs. 29.6 kg, p < 0.001) compared to those retained (Supplementary Table S1).

### Biological Aging Trajectory

Mean biological age decreased by −0.053 SD (SD = 0.485) over two years, indicating modest mean improvement across the sample. The standard deviation of Δbio-age (0.485) was substantially larger than the mean change, reflecting wide individual variability in biological aging trajectories. Baseline bio-age strongly predicted Δbio-age (r = −0.41), consistent with regression to the mean.

### Effect of Baseline SES on Biological Aging Trajectory

The overall CATE of low baseline SES on Δbio-age was +0.003 SD (bootstrap 95% CI: −0.025, +0.033; Table 1). Baseline SES was not associated with biological aging trajectory. This null finding was consistent across all prespecified subgroups: all CATE 95% confidence intervals crossed zero. The largest point estimates were observed in urban residents (CATE = +0.019; 95% CI: −0.039, +0.070) and low-grip participants (CATE = +0.010; 95% CI: −0.026, +0.051), but neither approached statistical significance (Table 1).

### SES × Physical Function Interaction

Cross-classification of baseline SES and grip strength showed that biological aging trajectory was differentiated by grip strength rather than SES (Table 2). Both low-grip groups showed substantial apparent improvement (Δbio-age ≈ −0.11 SD), while high-grip groups remained stable or showed slight worsening (Δbio-age ≈ −0.005 to +0.015 SD), consistent with regression to the mean. Within each grip stratum, the difference between low-SES and high-SES groups was negligible (0.01–0.02 SD), indicating no SES × grip interaction on biological aging trajectory.

### Mediation Through Depressive Symptoms

Baseline SES was not meaningfully associated with change in CES-D (path a: +0.170, indicating higher SES predicted slightly greater increase in depressive symptoms, though this effect was small relative to CES-D variability). Change in CES-D showed minimal association with change in biological age (path b: +0.002). The indirect effect of baseline SES on Δbio-age through ΔCES-D was +0.0004 (bootstrap 95% CI: −0.0005, +0.0015), with an estimated proportion mediated of 2.9% (bootstrap 95% CI: 0.1% to 76.8%). Both the total effect (+0.014; 95% CI: −0.010, +0.038) and direct effect (+0.014; 95% CI: −0.010, +0.038) had confidence intervals crossing zero.

### Sensitivity to Unmeasured Confounding

The E-value for the unadjusted ATE of low SES on Δbio-age was 1.05 (bootstrap 95% CI lower bound: 1.03). The E-value from the adjusted linear model was 1.13 (95% CI lower bound: 1.03). Both values indicate minimal robustness to unmeasured confounding—an unmeasured confounder associated with both SES and Δbio-age by a risk ratio of approximately 1.05–1.13 would be sufficient to explain the observed null association entirely.

## Discussion

In this longitudinal analysis of 4,658 Chinese older adults followed over two years, baseline SES did not predict biological aging trajectory. The overall CATE of low SES on two-year biological age change was +0.003 SD—effectively zero—with a narrow confidence interval (−0.025 to +0.033) that excludes any clinically meaningful effect in either direction. This null finding was consistent across all prespecified subgroups and was unchanged in sensitivity analyses using continuous SES and complete-case data.

These findings contrast with our earlier cross-sectional analysis of the same CHARLS wave, which identified a modest SES–biological age association (ATE = −0.118 SD) that was counterintuitive in direction (lower biological age in the low-SES group). The longitudinal design clarifies that this cross-sectional association likely reflects a combination of survivorship bias, reverse causation (biological aging → lower SES), and cohort-level confounding, rather than a causal effect of SES on the pace of biological aging. The contrast illustrates the well-known gap between cross-sectional associations and within-person change in aging research [28].

The absence of SES × grip interaction on biological aging trajectory is notable. Both low-grip groups showed apparent biological age improvement (−0.11 SD over two years) while high-grip groups remained stable, a pattern consistent with regression to the mean in the presence of measurement error. Within each grip stratum, however, SES did not differentiate trajectories. This finding does not support the double jeopardy hypothesis [18,22] for short-term biological aging change and suggests that the SES × grip interaction observed cross-sectionally in our data and others' [4,23] may be specific to the between-person level.

The null mediation through depressive symptom change is informative. While cross-sectional studies have reported substantial SES–CES-D associations in Chinese populations [17], the two-year change in CES-D was only weakly associated with SES and minimally associated with biological age change. This suggests that either the CES-D instrument does not capture the relevant dimensions of socioeconomic stress that influence biological aging rate, or that the biological pathways linking SES to aging operate over longer time scales than the two-year window examined here. Allostatic load and inflammatory pathways, which accumulate over decades, may be more relevant mediators [9,26,27].

Several limitations should be considered. First, the two-year follow-up may be too short to detect SES effects that manifest over longer periods; childhood SES, for example, has been shown to predict grip strength trajectories over decades in CHARLS [4]. Second, the 20.6% attrition rate, with older and physically frailer participants more likely to be lost, may introduce selection bias that attenuates SES effects if lower-SES participants with the fastest biological aging were differentially lost to follow-up. Third, the biological age composite lacks molecular markers (DNA methylation, inflammatory biomarkers) that may be more responsive to socioeconomic exposures [29]. Fourth, the median-split SES classification discards within-group variation. Fifth, CHARLS income data contain a high proportion of zero values, particularly among rural older adults.

These findings have implications for research and policy. The null result suggests that interventions targeting adult SES alone may not meaningfully alter short-term biological aging trajectories in older adults, and that physical function—particularly grip strength—is a more proximal marker of concurrent biological aging status and trajectory. Future work should employ longer follow-up periods, multiple repeated biomarker assessments, and cross-national harmonized data [5,34] to determine whether SES effects on biological aging rate emerge over longer time scales or are concentrated in specific life-course windows.

## Conclusions

In this longitudinal cohort of Chinese older adults, baseline socioeconomic status did not predict two-year biological aging trajectory. Biological aging change varied substantially across individuals, but this variation was not explained by SES, either overall or within subgroups defined by physical function, psychological distress, urban/rural residence, or age. Grip strength at baseline was a stronger correlate of biological aging trajectory than SES, though the observed pattern was consistent with regression to the mean. These findings suggest that the cross-sectional SES–biological aging association reported in prior studies may not reflect a causal effect of SES on the rate of biological aging, and underscore the importance of longitudinal designs for aging inequality research.
