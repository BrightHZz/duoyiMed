**Dear Editor,**

We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Prediction of 2-Year Frailty Worsening Using Machine Learning in the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study"** for consideration for publication in *Aging Clinical and Experimental Research*.

Frailty is a dynamic geriatric syndrome, and accurately identifying community-dwelling older adults at high risk of worsening is critical for targeted interventions. While machine learning (ML) models have shown promise in frailty prediction, their advantage over simpler, more interpretable linear models remains unclear, particularly in large, population-based Chinese cohorts. This study addresses this gap by developing and comparing ML and linear models for predicting 2-year frailty worsening.

Using data from 11,570 non-frail participants aged 60 years and older from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) 2013 wave, we found that both a linear (LASSO regression) and a non-linear (XGBoost) model achieved excellent and nearly identical discrimination (AUC 0.864 vs. 0.862, DeLong test P=0.41). Grip strength emerged as the single most important predictor. Our findings suggest that a parsimonious, linear model may be sufficient for risk stratification in this population, offering a practical and interpretable tool for clinical application.

We believe our work offers several novel contributions:

1. **First comprehensive prediction of Fried Phenotype frailty worsening** using the 2013 CHARLS wave, which uniquely includes grip strength measurements.
2. **Systematic comparison of LASSO and XGBoost**, demonstrating that a linear model achieves comparable performance to a complex ML model, challenging the assumption that "more complex is always better."
3. **Robust sensitivity analysis** accounting for the competing risk of death, a critical but often overlooked factor in geriatric prediction studies.
4. **Large-scale, population-based evidence** from a nationally representative Chinese cohort (N=11,570), enhancing the generalizability of our findings.

This manuscript is original, has not been published previously, and is not under consideration for publication elsewhere. All authors have read and approved the final version of the manuscript and agree with its submission to *Aging Clinical and Experimental Research*. We have no conflicts of interest to declare.

We believe our findings will be of great interest to your readership, particularly those focused on geriatric assessment, frailty management, and the application of predictive analytics in aging research.
